New York Divorce Proceedings for Abandoned Spouses

Before 2010, divorces in the state of New York required a spouse to demonstrate fault, such as desertion, on the part of their partner. However, in 2010 New York passed legislation that introduced no-fault divorce and now allows a spouse to file for divorce when they believe their marriage has irretrievably broken down for six months or more. This ground is less complicated and easier to prove than other fault grounds, such as abandonment, yet understanding abandoned spouse rights remains crucial in these proceedings.

Constructive Abandonment
New York state divorce laws define abandonment in two different ways: constructive and actual. A claim of constructive abandonment is when a spouse leaves the home with the intent to never return and without valid justification. This type of abandonment can be difficult to prove because a spouse must demonstrate that they left voluntarily and did not intend on returning. It also must be demonstrated that the spouse did not have a valid reason to leave, such as ill treatment or their consent. In cases of constructive abandonment, understanding abandoned spouse rights can significantly influence the outcome of the divorce proceedings.

A claim of actual abandonment is when a spouse does not have a good cause for leaving the home and does not make repeated attempts to request sexual relations with their spouse. This type of abandonment is more likely to be defended, particularly by the party who was abandoned, as they may argue that their spouse simply did not care for them or wanted them anymore. Here, too, abandoned spouse rights can provide essential legal leverage in court.

For a person to file for divorce on the grounds of either abandonment or constructive abandonment, they must first submit a petition to the court. This petition must include supporting documents such as a marriage certificate, financial information and any evidence that the couple has been living separately for more than a year. A hearing is held by the judge who will examine the evidence and decide whether to grant the divorce. Awareness of abandoned spouse rights is critical when preparing for such a hearing to ensure all relevant legal aspects are properly addressed.

The court will then establish the conditions of the divorce in a written decree. This will include the terms of property distribution, alimony and child custody. A contested divorce may take a long time to complete, and it can be expensive if there are disputed issues like child custody and maintenance. However, if a spouse can use the grounds of abandonment as their reasoning for filing for divorce, this may help speed up the process and reduce legal fees. By asserting abandoned spouse rights, the impacted partner may also be in a better position to negotiate more favorable terms.

During the divorce hearing, the court will discuss the details of the marriage and distribute the assets. This is called equitable distribution and it does not always mean a 50/50 split of the marital estate. Rather, the judge will consider all of the facts of the case and the impact on each spouse before determining what is fair and just. In such situations, the protection of abandoned spouse rights can ensure that the distribution is equitable, recognizing the challenges faced by the abandoned party.
